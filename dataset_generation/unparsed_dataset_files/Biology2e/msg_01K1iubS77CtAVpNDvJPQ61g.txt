    "question": "How does the cell make ATP during cellular respiration?",
    "answer": "Cells make the majority of their ATP during the electron transport chain and chemiosmosis in the last stage of cellular respiration. During the earlier steps, glucose is broken down into smaller molecules, and some energy is captured in ATP or electron carriers (NADH and FADH2). In the electron transport chain, those high-energy electrons are passed along a series of protein complexes in the mitochondrial inner membrane. This causes hydrogen ions (protons) to get pumped into the intermembrane space. The concentration gradient causes those protons to flow back into the mitochondrial matrix through an enzyme called ATP synthase. ATP synthase acts as a mini turbine, capturing the flow of protons to power the addition of a phosphate group to ADP, producing ATP. This process of harnessing the proton gradient to make ATP is called chemiosmosis. So in summary, most ATP is made via the electron transport chain creating a proton gradient that is used by ATP synthase to power ATP synthesis."

    "question": "Why is cellular respiration called an aerobic process?",
    "answer": "Cellular respiration is called an aerobic process because it requires oxygen. Specifically, the last stages of cellular respiration - the Krebs cycle and electron transport chain - can only occur if oxygen is present. But the bulk of ATP production happens in the oxygen-dependent Krebs cycle and electron transport chain, so cellular respiration as a whole is considered aerobic.
